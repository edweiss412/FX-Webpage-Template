The one idea

Tell one operator about a problem where they'd fix it, say it once, and say it in plain words.

Everything below is a consequence of that sentence. "One operator" is the person who runs FXAV — the shows come in as Google Sheets, and the app turns each one into crew webpages. When a sheet is broken, a video won't load, or two crew share an email, the app raises an alert. The whole system exists to make those alerts land in the right place, without noise, and without confusing anyone.

The four pillars

What "the system" actually is

Four rules, each enforced automatically. Together they are the philosophy.

Pillar 01

One record per problem

Each distinct problem on each show becomes exactly one alert. If the same fault happens a hundred times in a row, it stays one alert with a counter — not a hundred copies. It only goes away when a person acknowledges it (after fixing the cause) or the app detects the cause is gone.

Why it matters: the operator sees a true list of things to do, never a flood.

Pillar 02

A shared vocabulary — never a raw code on screen

Every kind of alert has an internal code and a matching plain-English message kept in one catalog. The screen always shows the friendly message, never the code. The wording is stored in three places for different parts of the app, and an automated check blocks any release where those three drift apart.

Why it matters: the operator always reads a sentence they understand, and the wording can't silently rot.

Pillar 03

Show it where the fix is

The newest and most opinionated rule. A diagram problem appears next to the diagrams; a video problem next to the video field; a sync problem at the top. A single count in the header is the at-a-glance signal. Every one of those numbers and markers comes from one shared calculation, so they can never disagree — and it is structurally impossible to raise an alert that has nowhere to appear.

Why it matters: the operator reads the problem and fixes it in the same spot, not two screens apart.

Pillar 04

The right person, and safe details

Alerts are sorted by audience — some are for the content owner, some for whoever watches the app's health. That sorting is about focus, not security. Two kinds of alert (app-wide vs. one-show) are cleared through different paths, and a one-show alert can't be dismissed without opening that show first, so there's always a record that someone looked. Any emails or secrets inside an alert are hidden by default and only revealed on purpose.

Why it matters: the right person sees each alert, and sensitive data doesn't leak into a dashboard.

Pillar 03, made concrete

Where an alert shows up

A simplified view of one show's admin page. The header carries the total; a problem tied to a specific thing sits beside that thing. A show-wide problem sits at the top, because its fix is show-wide too.

Show admin page needs attention
Overview Sheet & sync problems — the fix is one Re-sync button here show-wide
Crew A problem with a specific crew member by that person
Diagrams A venue image that couldn't be captured at the diagram
Opening reel The intro video won't play or isn't a video at the reel field
Warnings A parse notice — a sheet that fell back to its last good copy a banner line
Six destinations in all (the change log is the sixth). App-wide alerts — a broken sign-up sheet, a stalled sync — belong to the whole system and never attach to a single show; see the next section for where those go instead.

An honest detail: only about a dozen alert types today point at a specific spot — a crew member, a diagram, the reel field, a parse notice. The rest resolve to Overview. That is not a shortfall: their fix genuinely is show-wide (press Re-sync) or is a system fault with no single place on the page to sit beside. "Show it where the fix is" often is the top of the show.

The card parts: a filled amber ! badge · the plain sentence (what happened + what to do) · the ? that opens the "why this shows up" background · an optional detail band or repeat count · a footer with when it was raised and either a Confirm button, a self-clears note, or a link to the fix.

When several land on one crew member, the alert banners and the parse-warning cards for that person merge into a single stack beneath their row — capped at two visible, with the rest folded behind a "2 more" disclosure that opens in place. One person's problems stay together and never push the roster apart.

In the show's Warnings panel, a parse notice renders as a line, not a card

Crew are still seeing the last good version. Your latest changes didn't go through. No parseable date. Anything listed below is from the version crew can see, not from the change that failed.

Where it lives: at the top of the show's Warnings panel, above the individual warning rows, set off by a hairline. Real composed copy. No stripe, no ?, no Confirm button — parse notices are context, not a to-do, and the two warnings codes are the only alerts that render this way. "No parseable date" is the captured failure category (one of eight); the exact offending cell is deferred to the parse panel, not shown inline.

One tone, on purpose. Every alert wears the amber "worth a look" treatment. The louder red, can't-miss styling is held back for a different thing entirely — a blocking hold that stops a publish from going out. So the color itself carries meaning: amber says attention, red says stop. No alert ever cries wolf in red.

It's also why the Overview catch-all looks just like a precisely-placed alert — same card, stacked at the top rather than beside a piece of content. Nothing about it reads as "couldn't be placed." It reads as "here's the problem, and the fix is right here."

This is a to-do list, not a roster of every alert. Only items needing a human decision get a row: manual-fix alerts (amber dot) and pending change approvals (red dot — the one place red appears). Each row is a jump link to where the fix lives (Pillar 03), a 44px tap target with a bold title and "show · section" subtitle. Alerts that heal themselves never get a row — the footer just tallies them as "clearing." Inbox alerts don't appear here at all; they live in the bell.

The same count also drives the per-section rail dots down the side of the modal: a section with an open alert shows a filled amber dot, a clean one a hollow teal ring. Because the pill, the list, and every dot read from one calculation, the header total can never disagree with what's actually in the sections — Pillar 03's no-drift guarantee, made visible.

The other tier

App-wide alerts go somewhere else

A show alert lives on its show. But some problems belong to the whole system, not one show — so they never appear in a show's page at all. They surface on their own set of screens.

Where it appearsWhat lands there
Notification bellThe running inbox of things needing a look — the badge and dropdown in the admin nav.
Dashboard bannerA critical, can't-be-dismissed strip across the top of the admin home for the most serious faults.
Health panelA developer-only telemetry view that lists health faults across every show, regardless of which one.
Email digestApp-wide faults can also go out as an email, so no one has to be watching the screen.

Same underlying record, different doors. One app-wide fault can even show in more than one place — the bell for a quick glance, an email for the person who's away — because each alert is tagged with who it's for and how loud it should be. That tagging is Pillar 04 doing its job.

The voice

How the alerts are worded

The copy has its own house style, because a warning nobody understands is worse than none.

The reader model: the Google Sheet is what the show owner types in; the webpage is what the crew sees. Alert copy always speaks in those terms — "the sheet is missing a date," not a database field name.

The shape: a short, concrete sentence inline that says both what happened and what to do about it ("...replace the link with a video file to restore playback"). A small ? opens the background — why this shows up — and a Help link holds the deep version. No jargon, and a house ban on em-dashes and raw error codes.

Name the person, show the real fix. A card about one crew member names them and quotes the actual correction — "We read 'Strke' as 'Strike' in Eric Weiss's role" — instead of a generic template. And when an instruction wouldn't actually help (three of the auto-corrections re-apply on every sync no matter what the sheet says), the copy drops the dead-end "fix the sheet" line rather than sending the operator on an errand that can't work.

If it can't be fixed, it can be dismissed — for good. Those un-fixable notices aren't dead weight: every parse-warning card has a Report / Ignore control, and Ignore is remembered by the note's content, not a one-time id. So a warning ignored once stays tucked in the section's "Ignored" list across every future sync instead of nagging again — the durable acknowledgement that stands in for a fix. (Parse warnings are a sibling surface to alerts; only alerts use the amber card and Confirm flow, but the two share this modal.)

Why it won't rot

The guardrails that keep it honest

The philosophy isn't a document people are asked to remember — it's a set of automated tests that fail the build when a rule is broken. These are the load-bearing ones, in plain terms.

enforced in tests blocks release on drift new code fails by default
GuardrailWhat it guarantees
Catalog matchThe three copies of every alert's wording stay identical; a release is blocked the moment they disagree.
CoverageEvery alert type must have a place to render. Add a new one with no home and the build fails.
AudienceEvery alert is assigned an audience. An unrecognized one is shown to everyone rather than silently hidden.
Origin registryAny new spot in the code that raises an alert must declare who it's for and which show. Undeclared ones fail by default.
Action logEvery admin action that changes data leaves a durable record, so nothing happens in the dark.
The honest status

Converged, but not yet collected

Here's the nuance behind the one-word answer. The rules above have genuinely converged — they're consistent, and the test suite enforces them. What never existed is a single page that states them as a philosophy. Instead they're distributed across the master spec, a set of project-wide invariants, roughly two dozen smaller specs, and the tests themselves.

Those two dozen specs came in two waves. The nearest thing to a map before this page was a table buried in the routing spec listing every alert type and where it now renders.

Early July · wave one

The foundations

Auto-resolution, sorting alerts by audience, showing who an alert is about, the two clear-it paths, and the notification bell. This wave set the data model and the audience split.

Mid–late July · wave two

The surface and the voice

A unified attention surface, compact alert cards, the ? detail popover, a copy restore, and the routing rule — "show it where the fix is." This wave is where the plain-language, in-place philosophy took its current shape.

Now

This page

The first time the four pillars, the voice, and the guardrails sit in one place — so the next person doesn't have to re-derive them from two dozen documents.

Plain-language glossary

The five words that keep recurring

Alert
One flagged problem on a show (or app-wide) that needs a person's attention.
Code
The internal name for a kind of alert. Never shown to a human — it's the key that looks up the plain-English wording.
Catalog
The single list pairing every code with the message a person actually reads.
Attention surface
The parts of the admin screen where alerts appear — the header count plus the in-place markers by each section.
Resolve
To acknowledge an alert after fixing its cause. A deliberate "I handled this," not an undo.
